# Negotiation & Leveling — Interview Scenarios

<article data-difficulty="junior">

## 🟢 Junior: "What Are Your Salary Expectations?" in the First Call

**Scenario:** Five minutes into your first recruiter screen for a junior DE role, the recruiter asks: "So we don't waste anyone's time — what are your salary expectations?" You have no other offers, you researched a rough market range last night (65–85K for this city and level), and you really want this job. What do you say?

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint</summary>

Your goals in order: don't anchor low, don't get filtered out, extract *their* band if possible. There's a polite deflection that works most of the time, and a researched-range fallback when they insist. Think about which number of your range you'd state if forced — and why naming a single low number to "seem reasonable" backfires.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

**First move — deflect politely and flip it:**

> "At this stage I'm focused on whether the role and team are the right fit — I trust you pay competitively for the level. Could you share the band for this position?"

This works more often than juniors expect: many recruiters will state the band (and in several jurisdictions they're required to). If they share it, you respond to *their* number: "That range works for me — let's keep going."

**If they insist** ("we need a number to proceed"):

> "Based on market data for junior DE roles here, I'm seeing roughly 70–85K total. I'm flexible for the right team — where does this role's band sit relative to that?"

Mechanics:
- **Give a range whose bottom you'd actually be happy with** — the bottom is what they hear. Don't state 65 to "seem reasonable" if 65 would disappoint you.
- **Cite the source implicitly** ("market data") — it signals you'll notice a lowball without any threat.
- **End with a question** — keeps the information flowing toward you.

**What NOT to do:**
- State your current salary (deflect: "I'd rather anchor on this role's market value than my current package" — and in many places they can't require it).
- Give one precise low number out of anxiety.
- Say "whatever you think is fair" — that's an instruction to pay the band minimum.

**Why this matters even at junior level:** the number set here anchors the offer weeks later. Two sentences of preparation routinely move junior offers 5–10%.

</details>

## 🟡 Mid-Level: An Exploding Offer While Your First Choice Is Still Interviewing

**Scenario:** Company B (good, not your favorite) just made you an offer: 110K base, "valid for 72 hours." Company A (your first choice) has your final onsite scheduled in 6 days, with a typical 1-week decision turnaround after. The Company B recruiter says the deadline is firm because "we have other candidates." Walk through your options and execute the best play.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint</summary>

You have three levers: extend B's deadline (more available than it sounds), compress A's timeline (loops compress for candidates with offers weekly), and — only if both genuinely fail — decide with incomplete information. Think about what to tell each side, how honestly, and in what order. Also: what does B's behavior tell you if they truly won't extend?

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

**Play both levers simultaneously, fully honestly.**

**Move 1 — same hour, to Company A's recruiter:**

> "I've received an offer with a deadline this week. Your role is my first choice, and I don't want to make this decision without completing your process. Is there any way to compress — onsite in the next 2–3 days and a fast debrief?"

This works far more often than candidates believe: loops compress weekly for exactly this situation, and "you're my first choice" mobilizes recruiters. A's likely response: onsite moved up + expedited debrief, or at minimum an honest "we can't be ready in time" — which is also information.

**Move 2 — same day, to Company B:**

> "I'm seriously interested, and I want to say yes to an offer I'll be fully committed to. I'm completing one process that wraps early next week — can we extend to next Wednesday? If the answer is yes then, you'll have a wholehearted yes."

Extension requests framed as *commitment quality* (not shopping) are granted most of the time, once. 72-hour deadlines are negotiation devices more often than logistics.

**Decision tree for the outcomes:**

| A compresses? | B extends? | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Yes | Yes | Ideal: both resolve next week; negotiate with real comparison |
| Yes | No | Tell A the hard date; ask for verbal/conditional signal by B's deadline. If A can't signal, see below |
| No | Yes | Finish A's loop properly inside the extension |
| No | No | The genuine dilemma — decide on evidence you have |

**If you truly must decide blind:** weigh B's *behavior* as data — a firm artificial deadline that survives a polite, reasonable extension request is a (mild) culture signal. Then decide between "bird in hand at a good company" vs your honest read of A's probability and the roles' fit gap. Both choices are defensible; what's not defensible is accepting B while secretly planning to renege if A comes through — reneging after signing burns both bridges and travels in the industry.

**What you never do:** lie to either side, invent deadlines, or use A's name with B (or vice versa) without need. Specific company names add no leverage over "another process at final stage" — and discretion itself signals professionalism.

</details>

## 🔴 Senior: The Staff Offer With a Vague Charter and a Comp Gap

**Scenario:** After a strong loop, you receive a staff-level offer at a 600-person company: base 175K, equity worth ~60K/year on paper (private company, Series D), 15% bonus. Two issues: (1) your modeling of a competing public-company senior offer puts it ~25K/year ahead on reliable value, and (2) the staff role's scope was described differently by the hiring manager ("own the data platform") and the director ("help several teams with architecture") — a vagueness you flagged in your notes. You want this job *if it's real*. Negotiate the package and the role.

<details>
<summary>💡 Hint</summary>

Two negotiations, and the charter one matters more: a staff title without written scope is a senior job with extra meetings. Think about which conversation comes first, how to convert the scope ambiguity into offer-stage clauses (charter, reporting line, first-quarter mandate, review terms), and how to value Series-D paper honestly in the comp ask (dilution, liquidity, exercise terms) without insulting their equity story.

</details>

<details>
<summary>✅ Solution</summary>

**Sequence: resolve the role before the money.** Comp negotiation on an undefined role optimizes the wrong variable — and the charter conversation often *unlocks* the comp one.

**Conversation 1 — with the hiring manager (not the recruiter), framed as alignment:**

> "Before we finalize, I want to make sure we agree on what success looks like — I heard 'own the data platform' from you and 'support several teams' architecture' from [director]. Those are different jobs. Can we write down the charter: what I own, what I influence, and what my first two quarters' success criteria are?"

What you're asking to get **in writing** (offer letter, role doc, or a confirmed email — artifact matters more than format):
1. **Charter:** "Owns data platform architecture and roadmap across [orgs]" — ownership language, not "supports."
2. **Reporting line** and what happens to it in a reorg (staff roles die by reporting-line drift).
3. **First-quarter mandate:** the concrete problem (e.g., "reduce cross-team pipeline incident rate; establish platform contract standards").
4. **Resourcing:** if the pitch included team-building — headcount *with quarters attached*.

If the org can't converge on a written charter for a staff hire, that *is* the answer: you'd be accepting a title, not a role. Price accordingly or walk.

**Conversation 2 — comp, after the charter lands:**

Value the equity like an engineer, out loud but respectfully:

> "I've modeled both offers on 4-year reliable value. Yours: I'm applying a private-company discount to the equity — current 409A, dilution through a likely pre-IPO round, and no liquidity program means I can't weight the 60K/year at face. That puts your package ~25K/year behind on dependable value. The role is why I'm here — close part of that gap and structure the rest, and I'm done: specifically, base to 185K, and either a larger initial grant or a liquidity commitment in writing. If we get there, I sign this week."

Notes on the mechanics:
- **Ask their equity questions first** (shares outstanding, last 409A, tender history, exercise window) — the *answers* either justify their valuation or justify your discount, and asking signals you're pricing, not posturing.
- **Base + signing move easiest** at private companies (cash is budgeted; grant sizes often need committee). A 10K base move + 15–20K signing + a written 12-month comp review is a realistic landing zone for a 25K modeled gap.
- **Use the competing offer honestly and once** — numbers, framed as modeling, not as a threat.

**The integrated close:** charter in writing + ~70% of the comp gap closed + review terms for the remainder → sign. Charter refused or diluted → the public-company senior offer with clear scope is likely the better *job*, not just the better package — and saying so (warmly) in the decline is both true and leaves the door open at staff level when their org matures.

**What makes this answer senior:** sequencing role before money, converting interview-stage ambiguity into offer-stage clauses, pricing private equity with stated method rather than feelings, and treating "no written charter" as decision-grade information.

</details>

## Interview Tips

> **Tip 1:** When asked about expectations early, deflect once politely and ask for the band; if pressed, give a researched range whose bottom you'd happily accept, and cite "market data" — it moves anchors without any confrontation.

> **Tip 2:** Negotiate level before money, and scope before level: the band is set by the level, and at senior+ the written charter is worth more than the last 10% of comp. "Is this req leveled for the scope I'm targeting?" is a pre-loop question, not a post-offer complaint.

> **Tip 3:** All leverage is honest leverage: real competing offers, market data, and scope discovered during the loop. A called bluff doesn't just lose the raise — it taxes the trust you'll work under for years. One warm, specific, finite ask ("close X and I sign today") outperforms every multi-round haggle.

## ⚡ Quick-fire Q&A

**Q: Should I ever give the first number?**
A: Avoid it once politely ("could you share the band?"); if pressed, give a researched range whose bottom you'd genuinely accept — the bottom is what gets heard.

**Q: What's usually the most flexible component of an offer?**
A: Signing bonus, then start date and title; then equity grant size at senior levels. Base bands are stiffest, especially at large companies.

**Q: How do I value startup options honestly?**
A: Ask shares outstanding, last 409A/round valuation, exercise window, and liquidity history — then discount heavily (early stage ≈ 0 for decision purposes). Never trade guaranteed base for paper you can't price.

**Q: The offer is one level below what I interviewed for — what's the move?**
A: Ask what evidence the committee lacked, supply artifacts mapped to their rubric, and request a focused follow-up interview. If the level stands, negotiate band-top + a written 6-month review with criteria — or walk knowingly.

**Q: How do I use a competing offer without burning anyone?**
A: Accurately and once: "You're my first choice; the gap is X; close it and I sign today." Real numbers, no fabrication, and honor the commitment.

**Q: Is an exploding 48–72h deadline normal?**
A: It's common as a pressure device; a polite extension request framed around decision quality is granted most of the time. A refusal after a reasonable ask is itself culture data.

**Q: Should I accept a counteroffer from my current employer?**
A: Only if it fixes the reason you looked (scope, manager, direction) — counteroffers that fix only the number have poor 12-month retention outcomes, and the trust ledger changes on both sides.

**Q: What non-cash items are most worth negotiating?**
A: Written remote/hybrid terms, start date, title, review timing (6-month comp/level review with criteria), learning budget, and — at senior+ — the charter, reporting line, and headcount timing. Several outvalue a 5% base bump.
